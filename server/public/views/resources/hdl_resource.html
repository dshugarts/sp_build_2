Overview

Controversy exists to date on the precise and most effective means to reduce LDL and increase HDL levels in the blood. Despite the national focus on lowering, there is evidence to support that very-low levels of LDL cholesterol can be harmful to health too. Mostly, because the foods used to replace cholesterol-containing foods are either rich in sugar and/or pro-inflammatory agents, leading to metabolic disorders such as insulin resistance and atherosclerosis.

Nutrition

Traditional approaches to reduce cholesterol, specifically LDL, include a reduction in saturated fat intake (such as red meats) to about 10% of all calories consumed in a day and to avoid any trans-fats containing food. Additionally, individual wanting to manage cholesterol by reducing LDL and increasing HDL should focus on consuming polyunsaturated fats (such as olive and coconut oil), lean meats (such a fish and poultry), and reduce sugar intake to no more than 5% of all calories consumed in a day.
